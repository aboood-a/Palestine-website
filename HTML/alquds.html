Al-Quds, known in Arabic as Al-Quds, is one of the most ancient and sacred cities in the world. Its golden domes rise above stone streets, surrounded by olive trees and centuries of history. For thousands of years, Al-Quds has been a center of faith, culture, and beauty. It is a city where the call to prayer from Al-Aqsa Mosque echoes alongside the bells of churches, and where every stone carries the memory of generations.

Al-Quds has been inhabited for thousands of years and holds spiritual significance for Muslims, Christians, and Jews alike. For Palestinians, it has always been the heart of culture and identity. Before occupation, Al-Quds thrived with lively markets, schools, traditional crafts, and a rich social life. The Occupation of Al-Quds

Before the Occupation

Jerusalem has always been a major religious, cultural, and historical center. It holds deep significance for all three Abrahamic religions and has been a symbol of Palestinian identity for centuries. Before the occupation, Jerusalem thrived under Ottoman rule and later the British Mandate. During this time, Zionist ambitions increased, especially following the Balfour Declaration of 1917, which promised the establishment of a Jewish homeland in Palestine.

The Nakba and Western Jerusalem (1948)

In 1948, following the declaration of the State of the occupation and the outbreak of the Nakba war, the occupation forces occupied Western Jerusalem, including several historic Arab neighborhoods. Thousands of Palestinian families were forcibly expelled from their homes. Meanwhile, Eastern Jerusalem, including the Old City, Al-Aqsa Mosque, and the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, remained under Jordanian administration.

Eastern Quds Occupation (1967)

During the Six-Day War in June 1967, Israel attacked several Arab countries and occupied the remainder of Palestine, including Eastern Jerusalem. On June 7, 1967, the occupation's forces entered the Old City through the Lion‚Äôs Gate (Bab al-Asbat). After the Occupation

Entire Palestinian neighborhoods, such as Moroccan Quarter, were demolished to create sites like the Western Wall Plaza. Policies of land confiscation, identity restrictions, settlement construction, and demographic changes were implemented to alter the character of the city. üåü Sacred & Cultural Sites

Al-Aqsa Mosque:

The third holiest site in Islam, located in the Old City. It is a place of worship and spiritual significance for Muslims worldwide. Church of the Holy Sepulchre: A major Christian pilgrimage site believed to be the location of Jesus' crucifixion and resurrection.

Dome of the Rock:

An iconic Islamic shrine with its golden dome, located on the Temple Mount, it is a masterpiece of Islamic architecture.

The Old City:

A UNESCO World Heritage site, it is home to ancient streets, markets, and religious landmarks that tell the story of Al-Quds's diverse history.

Palestinians in Al-Quds face numerous challenges due to the ongoing occupation. Many live under the threat of home demolitions, restrictions on movement, and limited access to services. The Israeli government has implemented policies that make it difficult for Palestinians to maintain their residency status in the city.

Palestinians face:

Checkpoints and barriers that limit freedom of movement. Home demolitions in neighborhoods such as Sheikh Jarrah and Silwan. Restrictions on worship, where many Palestinians are denied access to Al-Aqsa. Settlement expansion, changing the character of Palestinian neighborhoods.

üë• Human Stories

Behind the walls and streets are families who have lived in Al-Quds for generations. Some were expelled in 1948, carrying only their keys and memories. Others remain, resisting pressure to leave. Their lives tell a story of strength, patience, and connection to the land.

ü™® Resilience & Hope

Despite decades of struggle, Al-Qudsites continue to preserve their identity. They protect their traditions through embroidery (tatreez), storytelling, prayer gatherings, and food like maqluba and kanafeh. Every Friday, thousands still gather at Al-Aqsa, proving that the spirit of Al-Quds cannot be erased.Despite the hardships, Al-Quds's Palestinians continue to hold onto their culture, traditions, and faith. Community organizations work tirelessly to support families, preserve heritage, and advocate for rights. Festivals, art, and education are vibrant parts of life in Al-Quds, showcasing the enduring spirit of its people.
